Why Teeth Stain

Common Causes of Discoloration

Understanding what causes stains helps us choose the right whitening approach for your unique smile.

Food & Beverages

Coffee, tea, red wine, and dark berries contain pigments called chromogens that attach to tooth enamel.

Tobacco Use

Tar and nicotine create stubborn, deep yellow or brown stains that are difficult to remove with brushing alone.

Natural Aging

As the outer enamel thins over time, the naturally yellowish inner layer (dentin) becomes more visible.

Science of Sparkle

How Professional Whitening Works

Teeth whitening works through a sophisticated chemical process. We apply a concentrated whitening gel (usually hydrogen peroxide) which breaks down into oxygen molecules.

These oxygen molecules penetrate the porous surface of the enamel to reach the discolored molecules in the dentin. By breaking the chemical bonds that hold stains together, the "stain molecules" become smaller and less pigmented, resulting in a significantly whiter tooth color.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is teeth whitening permanent?

No cosmetic whitening is permanent. However, with professional treatment and good maintenance, results can last from 12 to 24 months before a simple touch-up is needed.

Does whitening hurt?

Most patients feel zero pain. Some may experience temporary sensitivity to cold for 24–48 hours, which is normal and subsides quickly.

Will it whiten my fillings or crowns?

No. Whitening only works on natural tooth structure. If you have visible fillings or crowns, we will discuss how to match them to your new, brighter shade.

How white will my teeth get?

Most patients see an improvement of 6 to 10 shades. We use a shade guide to help you choose a result that looks brilliant but still natural for your features.

Can I drink coffee after whitening?

We recommend a "white diet" (avoiding colored foods/drinks) for the first 48 hours while your enamel's pores are closing for the best long-term results.

Is it safe for my enamel?

Yes. Professional whitening has been studied for decades and is proven to be safe for teeth when performed under clinical supervision.
